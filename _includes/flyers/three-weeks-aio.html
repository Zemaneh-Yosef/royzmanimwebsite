Sunset, the complete setting of the solar sphere in the west,[1] begins [the transition into] the new Halachic day. Thus, the start of the three weeks on and the start of the Tisha Be'Av fast on will happen at sunset.[2]

All other Rabbinical fasts begin from the moment one goes to sleep; however, a stipulation made before sleeping can negate that until Halachic Dawn.[3] Dawn marks the beginning of the morning's twilight, whose standard length is legislated as 72 minutes long.[4] This length is subject to change depending on the distance from the equator ("standard deviation") as well as the length of the day ("seasonal minutes").[5]

We are stringent for fasts to consider Halachic Nightfall only after 20 adjusted-seasonal minutes have passed since sunset[6] - there is no need to accommodate any other stringency for any fast (with the exception of Yom Kippur), even in the United States. The changes made to Dawn ("standard deviation", "seasonal minutes") are also included here, measured relative to the short time of 13.5/20

[1]: Halacha Berurah, intro. to Chapter 261 Halacha 21
[2]: See Ḥazon Ovadia, Arba Ta'aniyot, pg. 275-278 who recommends a 1-2 minute precaution to ensure no oversteps occur
[3]: Shulḥan Arukh O.Ḥ 562
[4]: Halikhot Olam Vol. I pg. 106
[5]: Halacha Berurah, intro. to Chapter 261, Halacha 13
[6]: Ḥazon Ovadia, Arba Ta'aniyot, pg. 12. This reflects the Rambam's view
